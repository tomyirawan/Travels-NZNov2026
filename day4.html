Tussock, Honey
& the Long Soak

Roll out of the alpine basin and trade glaciers for golden tussock. Crest the otherworldly 971-metre Lindis Pass, drop into Bannockburn for an organic pinot lunch at Carrick Winery, taste honesty-box Viper's Bugloss honey, graze the Central Otago fruit belt at Jones Family Fruit Stall, and roll into Queenstown in time for a 19:30 magnesium soak at Aluume hot pools — two minutes from your campground, while alpenglow lights the Crown Range.

Itinerary · Hour by Hour

From alpine basin to lake-edge.

A long day, but unhurried — the morning is alpine driving, lunch is organic pinot in Bannockburn, the afternoon is honey and fruit, and the evening is a hot soak in central Queenstown while alpenglow lights the Crown Range. The only hard time constraint is Carrick's 3 pm kitchen close; everything else has plenty of slack.

PACK · BREW

Coffee, tank check, packing — Glentanner Park Centre

3388 Mt Cook Rd, Glentanner · Google Map ↗

Slow start. Make a flat-white at the camp kitchen, top up the freshwater tank, dump grey water, and stow the night before's washing. Glentanner's open kitchen has the last decent espresso machine you'll see until Tarras — use it.

Pre-departure motorhome checklist Gas off · windows shut · roof vent down · awning rolled · cab seats rotated forward · loose jars (honey, salmon, last night's leftovers) stowed in low cupboards. The Lindis corners will rattle anything loose.

DRIVE

Roll south — SH80 → Pukaki → SH8 to Twizel

SH80 south to SH8 junction at Lake Pukaki · Route ↗

Last long glances at Aoraki/Mt Cook in the rear-view mirror. The road traces Lake Pukaki's west shore — the turquoise is more intense in morning light than at any other time of day. If you didn't get the Peters Lookout shot yesterday, pull in for one last frame at the southern end of the lake before SH8.

Once past the Pukaki junction you swing east-south-east and the landscape pivots from glacial blue to dry Mackenzie yellow.

FUEL · MUST

Diesel up — McKeown CardFuel 24/7, Twizel

1 Ostler Rd, Twizel · 24/7 unmanned high-flow · Google Map ↗

Fill the tank fully here. McKeown's unmanned card pumps run roughly 15–25¢/L cheaper than Cromwell and 30–40¢/L cheaper than Queenstown — and they're the last cheap diesel before you cross the Lindis. Pre-pay at the kiosk (set a $200 max-fill or a fixed amount), select your pump, swipe card, fuel. Refunds for unused balance hit the card within a few days.

Why here, not Cromwell Otago fuel jumps about 20¢/L the moment you cross into wine country. A Maui River burns roughly 39 L on the full Glentanner → Queenstown run — fill 60 L at Twizel prices and you save NZ$12–18 vs. topping up at Cromwell, plus the Twizel pumps are accessible 24/7 with no queue.

Leg-stretch — Omarama

Omarama township, SH8/SH83 junction · Google Map ↗

The last village before the climb. Pull in at the public toilets behind the domain, use them (Lindis Pass has none for the next 90 min), and stretch the passengers' legs. If anyone wants a hot drink, the Wrinkly Rams café does decent flat whites and home-baked muffins.

DRIVE · CLIMB

The Lindis climb — Omarama → Lindis Pass summit

SH8 / Omarama–Lindis Pass Rd · Route ↗

One of the most photogenic drives on the South Island. The road winds up through the Ahuriri valley, through golden tussock that catches the light in the most improbable ways, and tops out at 971 m — the highest sealed pass on a NZ state highway. The Maui River will pull noticeably in second/third gear on the climb; expect 14–16 L/100km on this stretch alone.

What to watch for Sheep on the verge, slow campervans (be one), and the moment the road crests and the whole Otago basin opens up to the south. Cameras ready.
11:30
30 min
MUST · LOOKOUT

Lindis Pass Summit Lookout — 971 m a.s.l.

Omarama–Lindis Pass Rd · small carpark on the right at the summit · Google Map ↗

Pull into the gravel carpark just past the summit cairn. A signed dirt path leads 200 m to a viewing platform — about a 10-minute easy walk. The platform view is fine, but the real view is from 50 m further up the slope beside it: scramble the unmarked sheep track for another 5 minutes and you get the full 360° of waves of tussock-gold ridges rolling south toward Otago. Pure High Country.

Photo angle Best frame: stand on the up-slope side and shoot south so the road snakes through gold tussock toward distant blue mountains. Mid-day light is harsh — if you want moody, come back at sunrise or sunset (you won't, but it's nice to know).
Motorhome parking The summit carpark is small (~5–7 cars) and gravel. If full, the Lindis Pass Viewpoint 200 m further south has a bigger pull-out and the same view. Don't park on the road shoulder — locals do 100 km/h through here.

Quick photo — Shrek the Sheep, Tarras

Tarras village, SH8 · Google Map ↗

The village is tiny — one café, one general store, one gift shop, and a bronze statue of Shrek the Sheep, the famously un-shorn merino who grew 27 kg of fleece on a nearby farm. Park briefly, jump out for the photo, use the public toilets, and roll on. Through the fruit belt to Bannockburn — Tarras → Carrick

SH8 south to Cromwell → SH8B over the Clutha dam → Bannockburn → Cairnmuir Rd · Route ↗

The road threads through the Cromwell fruit basin: cherry, apricot, peach, nectarine, and apple orchards laid out in long rows against the dry hills. Carrick sits 4 km up Cairnmuir Rd, signposted from the village.

MUST · LUNCH & WINE

Lunch — Carrick Winery Restaurant, Bannockburn

247 Cairnmuir Rd, Bannockburn · open 11 am–4 pm daily · Google Map ↗

A certified organic winery on the south slopes of Bannockburn, and one of the most lauded restaurants in Central Otago. The dining room and terrace look across the home vineyard to the Cairnmuir range — schist slopes, golden tussock, gnarled vines on terraces cut into the hillside. The food is paddock-to-plate modern NZ: small sharing plates and mains designed to pair with their pinot. Order the house pâté (cult-status among regulars), the duck, and the burrata if it's on. Wine flights ~NZ$30 pp, mains ~NZ$38–48.

The cellar door (just down from the restaurant) also does standalone tasting flights of 4–5 estate wines for ~NZ$15–20 pp — redeemable against a bottle purchase. Their Bannockburn Pinot Noir is the headline; the Riesling and unwooded Chardonnay are quietly excellent and rarely ordered. Pick up a bottle of the Bannockburn pinot to drink at tonight's camp-kitchen pre-soak meal.

Book ahead Carrick is busier on a Tuesday than you'd expect — locals, wine tours, occasional conferences. Reserve online or call +64 3 445 3480 in advance for a 13:30 table. Kitchen takes last orders at 3 pm; restaurant closes 4 pm.
Driver's tasting The driver can do a non-alcohol tasting of their organic grape juices, or one or two 15 ml pours of pinot — well under the NZ 0.05 BAC limit if spaced over a 100-min lunch with food. Save the proper drink for tonight's dinner.

MUST · HONEY

The Local Honey Shed — Bannockburn, Cromwell

230 Bannockburn Rd, Cromwell · open 9 am–6 pm daily · Google Map ↗

A small honesty-box shed in the Bannockburn vineyard country, run by local apiarists Tim and Zoe. Four headline varieties on the shelves: Mānuka (the famous antibacterial one), Rātā (creamy, from native rātā trees), Wild Thyme (Central Otago's flowering thyme on the dry hillsides — herbaceous, savoury, unique to this region), and the prized Viper's Bugloss — a pale, almost-white honey from a blue-flowered wildflower that's nearly impossible to find anywhere else.

No staff on site — it's a self-service shed with tasting spoons, marked prices, and an honesty till. Pay by card on the EFTPOS machine (PayWave works), or by phone bank transfer using the number on the wall if you need exact change. Buy a 500 g jar of the Viper's Bugloss; it's the one your friends back home will not be able to find.

Packing the jars They're glass. Wrap in a tea towel and stow low in a cupboard before driving back through the Kawarau Gorge — the twists will rattle anything loose.

MUST · FRUIT

Jones Family Fruit Stall — Kawarau Gorge Rd, Cromwell

489 SH6 (Kawarau Gorge Rd), Cromwell · open 8:30 am–6 pm daily · Google Map ↗

The most famous fruit stall in New Zealand. The Jones family have been growing here for four generations and the indoor barn-shop is a wall-to-wall riot of stone fruit, apples, pears, asparagus, jams, dried fruit, honey, fudge — and ice cream so good people queue out the door. Order a real-fruit ice cream (frozen fruit blended into vanilla soft-serve) to eat in the carpark — boysenberry or whichever stone fruit is in season is the move.

Buy tonight's pre-soak meal ingredients here: NZ asparagus, salad greens, fresh tomatoes, a punnet of stone fruit for dessert. Pre-cherry season in early November — but they'll still have last year's frozen cherries and Central Otago cherry jam. Saves a supermarket stop in Queenstown.

Practical Big gravel carpark, easy for a 7 m motorhome. Card-only at the checkout (no cash). Public toilets out back. Take your time — no time pressure for the rest of the day.

DRIVE · FINAL

Final leg — Jones Fruit Stall → Hampshire Lakeview

SH6 west through Kawarau Gorge → Gibbston Valley → Frankton → Queenstown CBD · Route ↗

The most dramatic stretch of the day. The road follows the Kawarau River through a narrow schist gorge — turquoise rapids, vine-terraced slopes, decommissioned gold-mining shanties on the cliffs. You'll pass the historic Kawarau suspension bridge, the original 1880 gold-rush crossing — slow down for the view but no need to stop. Then the road climbs out of the gorge and runs along the south-facing slopes of the Gibbston Valley — New Zealand's highest-altitude wine region, pinot noir on schist scree, vines on both sides for 8 km.

One more climb up the Kawarau slope and the whole Wakatipu basin opens up: first glimpse of Lake Wakatipu in the distance, the Remarkables running south. Stunning view; don't crash. Drop down through Frankton, follow SH6A along the lakefront into Queenstown CBD, right onto Brecon St, left onto Cemetery Rd.

Take it slow through the gorge Kawarau Gorge corners are tight for a 7 m motorhome and locals overtake aggressively. You've got plenty of time for a 19:30 hot pool — there's no need to rush. Use the designated slow-vehicle pull-outs to let traffic past.

CHECK IN

Hampshire Holiday Park — Queenstown Lakeview

4 Cemetery Rd, Queenstown · reception 8 am–7 pm · Google Map ↗

The most central holiday park in Queenstown — 10 minutes walk to the lakefront, 5 minutes to the Skyline Gondola, and (crucially) 2 minutes walk to Aluume hot pools. Reception closes at 7 pm, well clear of your 17:20 arrival. Get parked, hooked up to power, and changed before the pre-soak meal.

Motorhome site tips Some powered sites are tight for a 7 m van — when checking in, ask for a corner pitch or one along the back fence, where the manoeuvring room is best. Levelling blocks may be needed; the ground slopes gently. Dump station is on the way out, do it tomorrow morning.
Confirm tonight's Aluume booking Once parked, walk the 2 min to Aluume reception and confirm your 19:30 slot is on the books. If you didn't pre-book and there's space, grab a private pool now while availability is visible.

PRE-SOAK · LIGHT

Pre-soak meal — camp kitchen

Shared kitchen on-site

Light only — this is fuel, not the main event. A heavy meal in the 60 min before a 38°C soak loads your cardiovascular system unnecessarily and makes the bath uncomfortable. Aim for something easy: bread, cheese, the Jones salad and tomatoes, a few slices of cold meat, an apricot or two. Skip the wine for now — alcohol before a hot soak is a bad combination.

Drink a full 500 ml of water during this meal. You're about to lose a lot of it to the pool.

Why not the full Carrick-pinot feast yet Sitting down to a proper meal with wine at 6 pm, then trying to soak at 7:30, is a recipe for sluggish digestion and a poor pool experience. Save the real dinner for post-soak (~20:45) when your body's recovered.

WALK · 2 MIN

Walk to the hot pools — Hampshire → Aluume

Down Cemetery Rd, turn left onto Brecon St · 250 m · Route ↗

The whole reason you booked the Lakeview branch over any other Queenstown park. Aluume sits on the corner of Brecon and Cemetery — visible from your campground gate. Bring swimwear (issued towels available, but BYO towel saves $5), a water bottle, and slip-on shoes. Wine stays in the motorhome — alcohol isn't permitted at the pools and they'll notice.

19:30
60 min
MUST · SOAK

Bathe by Aluume — urban hot pools

34 Brecon St, Queenstown · open 9 am–11 pm daily · Google Map ↗

Queenstown's newest hot pool complex (opened Nov 2025) — handcrafted concrete tubs filled with magnesium-rich water at 38–39 °C, hidden behind a serpentine corten-steel wall on Brecon Street. The design references traditional Japanese onsen but the architecture is sharply modern: poured concrete, native planting, open sky overhead, mountain silhouettes behind. At 19:30 you'll catch the alpenglow on the Crown Range — the brief pre-sunset window when the peaks turn pink-gold (sunset is ~20:40 on 10 Nov 2026).

Two options:

Communal pool — NZ$55 pp, 60 min, ages 16+, up to 8 bathers (often fewer in shoulder season Tuesday evenings). Best view on-site, looks out toward the mountain silhouettes. Most social option.

Private pool — NZ$95 (1 bather) / NZ$165 (2) / NZ$215 (3) / NZ$250 (4), 60 min, ages 5+ (children 5–11 with paying adult). Three styles: enclosed Canopy, semi-enclosed Mountain View, semi-enclosed Botanical View. The Mountain View is the one to ask for — best alpenglow window.

Included: large plush towels, robes, outdoor shower amenities (Aluume's own shampoo / conditioner / body wash), unlimited drinking water, herbal tea after the soak. Sessions are exactly 60 min including changing and wind-down — they'll intercom you 5 min before time's up.

Pre-book online · aluume.com Walk-ins accepted "if there's space," but Tuesday-evening sunset slots in November are the most-requested time at Aluume. Book the 19:30 Mountain View private pool online a week or more out for safety; cancellation is free with 24 h notice for individuals.
Hydration matters A long drive at altitude + a 38°C soak = dehydration. Drink 500 ml water before and 500 ml after. The included herbal tea after the soak counts.

WALK · BACK

Walk back — Aluume → Hampshire

Brecon St → Cemetery Rd · 250 m back to the motorhome

Walk the 250 m back in your robes (Aluume lets you wear them out — most guests do). The post-soak walk in the cool Queenstown evening air is half the experience. Sunset is at 20:40 — if you stop on Brecon St you'll see the last of the alpenglow fading on the Remarkables to the south.

20:45
75 min
DINNER · FEAST

Camp-kitchen feast — Hampshire Lakeview

Shared kitchen / outdoor BBQ on-site

Now the real meal. Body's recovered from the soak, sleep is the goal, and the Carrick Bannockburn Pinot Noir is waiting. Char the rest of the Jones asparagus, pan-fry a steak or salmon fillet (pre-bought from a Queenstown supermarket en-route, or pick up earlier from New World on Gorge Rd if you didn't stock the fridge today), toss the salad with a Viper's Bugloss honey-mustard dressing, finish with stone fruit and the real-fruit ice cream you saved from Jones.

The unhurried meal matters more than the food — it's been a 12-hour day and the soak has dropped your heart rate into bedtime range. Eat slowly. One glass of pinot is plenty (you're rehydrating, not loading on alcohol).

If you're too relaxed to cook Brecon Street has takeaway 5 min walk away — Erik's Fish & Chips (cheap, from a converted caravan), Fergburger (legendary but 30-min queue), or Devil Burger for a faster alternative. Order takeaway and bring it back to eat in the camp.

Your bed

The day's done. You crossed the South Island spine, stood at 971 m on a tussock pass, ate organic duck and pinot in a Bannockburn vineyard, bought four jars of impossible honey, and ended the afternoon in a heated concrete tub under Queenstown alpenglow. Sleep should be exceptional.

Best station on route: McKeown CardFuel 24/7 — Twizel. Unmanned high-flow truck diesel pump, 24/7, accepts international cards (rare for self-serve in NZ). Pre-paid kiosk system — set $200 max-fill, unused balance refunds within 2–5 days. Roughly 15–25¢/L cheaper than the nearest Cromwell pump.

Backup if Twizel is unreachable: NPD Cromwell (167 McNulty Rd, 6 am–10 pm). Still cheaper than Queenstown; reliable card pumps.

Avoid filling in Queenstown — Frankton stations run 25–40¢/L more than Twizel. Tomorrow's Queenstown driving will burn maybe 5 L; you don't need to fill again until Day 6.

If you fall behind

Skip Clay Cliffs detour — saves 25 min if running late from Omarama.

Skip Tarras photo stop — drive straight through to Carrick to protect the 13:30 reservation. Kitchen takes last orders at 3 pm.

Skip the Honey Shed only if you arrive at Carrick after 14:30 — better to keep the lunch full than skip an iconic stop. You can still buy similar honey at New World in Queenstown (though no Viper's Bugloss).

Worst case: if check-in slips past 7 pm, call Hampshire (+64 3 442 7252) before reception closes so they can leave keys/site details in a late-arrival envelope.

Most places in the world, a road like this would have one thing on it. In Central Otago, you can crest a tussock pass at lunch, eat organic duck and pinot beside a schist vineyard at half-past one, taste honesty-box Viper's Bugloss honey at three, walk through four generations of fruit-growing at four, and finish the day in a magnesium hot pool while alpenglow lights the Crown Range — all on a single Tuesday.
