South Island Odyssey · Leg Four · ≈263 km

Tussock, Honey
& the Big Drop

Roll out of the alpine basin and trade glaciers for golden tussock. Crest the otherworldly 971-metre Lindis Pass, graze the Central Otago fruit belt at Jones Family Fruit Stall, taste honesty-box Viper's Bugloss honey in Bannockburn, watch strangers leap 43 metres off the world's first commercial bungy bridge, sneak a Gibbston pinot, and roll into Queenstown by way of a converted shearing-shed honey centre. Sleep two minutes from the lakefront.

Itinerary · Hour by Hour

From alpine basin to lake-edge.

A long but unhurried day, sequenced around hard closing times: Kawarau bungy stops at 4 pm, Gibbston cellar doors at 5 pm, and Buzzstop café shuts at 4 pm Tuesday. A 9 am roll-out from Glentanner makes everything fit with daylight to spare.

08:00
60 min
PACK · BREW

Coffee, tank check, packing — Glentanner Park Centre

3388 Mt Cook Rd, Glentanner · Google Map ↗

Slow start. Make a flat-white at the camp kitchen, top up the freshwater tank, dump grey water, and stow the night before's washing. Glentanner's open kitchen has the last decent espresso machine you'll see until Tarras — use it.

Pre-departure motorhome checklist Gas off · windows shut · roof vent down · awning rolled · cab seats rotated forward · loose jars (honey, salmon, last night's leftovers) stowed in low cupboards. The Lindis corners will rattle anything loose.
09:00
45 min · 55 km
DRIVE

Roll south — SH80 → Pukaki → SH8 to Twizel

SH80 south to SH8 junction at Lake Pukaki · Route ↗

Last long glances at Aoraki/Mt Cook in the rear-view mirror. The road traces Lake Pukaki's west shore — the turquoise is more intense in morning light than at any other time of day. If you didn't get the Peters Lookout shot yesterday, pull in for one last frame at the southern end of the lake before SH8.

Once past the Pukaki junction you swing east-south-east and the landscape pivots from glacial blue to dry Mackenzie yellow.

09:45
15 min
FUEL · MUST

Diesel up — McKeown CardFuel 24/7, Twizel

1 Ostler Rd, Twizel · 24/7 unmanned high-flow · Google Map ↗

Fill the tank fully here. McKeown's unmanned card pumps run roughly 15–25¢/L cheaper than Cromwell and 30–40¢/L cheaper than Queenstown — and they're the last cheap diesel before you cross the Lindis. Pre-pay at the kiosk (set a $200 max-fill or a fixed amount), select your pump, swipe card, fuel. Refunds for unused balance hit the card within a few days.

Why here, not Cromwell Otago fuel jumps about 20¢/L the moment you cross into wine country. A Maui River burns roughly 39 L on the full Glentanner → Queenstown run — fill 60 L at Twizel prices and you save NZ$12–18 vs. topping up at Cromwell, plus the Twizel pumps are accessible 24/7 with no queue.

30 min · 30 km
DRIVE

SH8 south — Twizel → Omarama

SH8 along the Wairepo flats and Ahuriri valley · Route ↗

Wide-open flats, the Ben Ohau range to your right, willow-lined irrigation canals on the left. About 8 km south of Twizel you'll pass High Country Salmon's floating café on a canal — skip the stop today (we'll buy honey-glazed salmon in Queenstown), but it's a nice scenery marker.

10:30
20 min
OPTIONAL · STRETCH

Leg-stretch — Omarama

Omarama township, SH8/SH83 junction · Google Map ↗

The last village before the climb. Pull in at the public toilets behind the domain, use them (Lindis Pass has none for the next 90 min), and stretch the passengers' legs. If anyone wants a hot drink, the Wrinkly Rams café does decent flat whites and home-baked muffins.

Optional · 25 min detour If you have time, drive 4 km up Omarama Station Rd to the Clay Cliffs — wind-eroded badlands pinnacles on private land (gold-coin gate fee). It's an unsealed road; fine for the motorhome at low speed. Skip if you're tight on time.
11:00
30 min · 30 km
DRIVE · CLIMB

The Lindis climb — Omarama → Lindis Pass summit

SH8 / Omarama–Lindis Pass Rd · Route ↗

One of the most photogenic drives on the South Island. The road winds up through the Ahuriri valley, through golden tussock that catches the light in the most improbable ways, and tops out at 971 m — the highest sealed pass on a NZ state highway. The Maui River will pull noticeably in second/third gear on the climb; expect 14–16 L/100km on this stretch alone.

What to watch for Sheep on the verge, slow campervans (be one), and the moment the road crests and the whole Otago basin opens up to the south. Cameras ready.
11:30
30 min
MUST · LOOKOUT

Lindis Pass Summit Lookout — 971 m a.s.l.

Omarama–Lindis Pass Rd · small carpark on the right at the summit · Google Map ↗

Pull into the gravel carpark just past the summit cairn. A signed dirt path leads 200 m to a viewing platform — about a 10-minute easy walk. The platform view is fine, but the real view is from 50 m further up the slope beside it: scramble the unmarked sheep track for another 5 minutes and you get the full 360° of waves of tussock-gold ridges rolling south toward Otago. Pure High Country.

Photo angle Best frame: stand on the up-slope side and shoot south so the road snakes through gold tussock toward distant blue mountains. Mid-day light is harsh — if you want moody, come back at sunrise or sunset (you won't, but it's nice to know).
Motorhome parking The summit carpark is small (~5–7 cars) and gravel. If full, the Lindis Pass Viewpoint 200 m further south has a bigger pull-out and the same view. Don't park on the road shoulder — locals do 100 km/h through here.
12:00
45 min · 50 km
DRIVE · DESCENT

Down to Tarras — Lindis Pass → Tarras village

SH8 south, descending into Lindis Valley then Tarras flats · Route ↗

The south side of the pass descends gently through a long valley dotted with merino runs. Around Lindis Crossing the country opens out completely — you're in Central Otago wine and stone-fruit country now, and the air goes dry and bright.

12:45
75 min
MUST · LUNCH

Lunch — Tarras Country Café

2792 Tarras–Cromwell Rd, Tarras · open 9 am–3 pm daily · Google Map ↗

A cult roadside café in a village so small it has one café, one general store, one gift shop, and a statue of Shrek the Sheep (the famously un-shorn merino, who came from a Tarras farm and grew 27 kg of fleece). Try the lamb panini or the cheese roll — a South Island classic of toasted bread rolled around onion-cheese filling. Vegan/vegetarian options are unusually good for a village this size.

Park the motorhome in the gravel lot behind the cafe (plenty of room) and wander next door to the Shrek Statue for a photo, then the gift shop — the merino blanket throws make decent souvenirs.

Timing note Tarras Country Café closes at 3 pm sharp. A 12:45 arrival gives you 75 min to eat, wander, use the toilets, and roll out by 14:00 with everything still open.
14:00
30 min · 32 km
DRIVE

Through the fruit belt — Tarras → Cromwell

SH8 / Tarras–Cromwell Rd through the orchard country · Route ↗

The road threads through the Cromwell fruit basin: cherry, apricot, peach, nectarine, and apple orchards laid out in long rows against the dry hills. In early November the cherry blossoms are mostly gone but the trees are heavy with green fruit — the first apricots will be a fortnight away. Watch for the big roadside Big Fruit sculpture at Cromwell's edge — it's worth a drive-by photo.

14:30
40 min
MUST · FRUIT

Jones Family Fruit Stall — Kawarau Gorge Rd, Cromwell

489 SH6 (Kawarau Gorge Rd), Cromwell · open 8:30 am–6 pm daily · Google Map ↗

The most famous fruit stall in New Zealand. The Jones family have been growing here for four generations and the indoor barn-shop is a wall-to-wall riot of stone fruit, apples, pears, asparagus, jams, dried fruit, honey, fudge — and ice cream so good people queue out the door. Order a real-fruit ice cream (frozen fruit blended into vanilla soft-serve) — try boysenberry or whichever stone fruit is in season.

Buy a punnet of whatever's seasonal for tonight's motorhome dessert. Early November means very early apricots, late hothouse strawberries, last of the winter apples, and excellent dried fruit. Pre-cherry season — but they'll still have last year's frozen cherries and Central Otago cherry jam.

Practical Big gravel carpark, easy for a 7m motorhome. Card-only at the checkout (no cash). Public toilets out back. The pricing is honest — comparable to or cheaper than New World supermarkets, with fresher fruit and better varieties.
15:10
15 min · 10 km
DRIVE · DETOUR

Detour to Bannockburn — Cromwell → Bannockburn Rd

SH6 north into Cromwell town → SH8B over the dam → right onto Bannockburn Rd · Route ↗

A short scenic detour. Cross the Clutha at the Cromwell Dam, swing right at the Bannockburn turn, and drive 5 km into the wine country at the south of Lake Dunstan. This is one of NZ's most distinctive landscapes: gold ridges scarred with old gold-mining sluice channels, vines hugging the slopes.

15:25
25 min
MUST · HONEY

The Local Honey Shed — Bannockburn, Cromwell

230 Bannockburn Rd, Cromwell · open 9 am–6 pm daily · Google Map ↗

A small honesty-box shed in the Bannockburn vineyard country, run by local apiarists Tim and Zoe. Four headline varieties on the shelves: Mānuka (the famous antibacterial one), Rātā (creamy, from native rātā trees), Wild Thyme (Central Otago's flowering thyme on the dry hillsides — herbaceous, savoury, unique to this region), and the prized Viper's Bugloss — a pale, almost-white honey from a blue-flowered wildflower that's nearly impossible to find anywhere else.

No staff on site — it's a self-service shed with tasting spoons, marked prices, and an honesty till. Pay by card on the EFTPOS machine (PayWave works), or by phone bank transfer using the number on the wall if you need exact change. Buy a 500 g jar of the Viper's Bugloss; it's the one your friends back home will not be able to find.

Detour cost · ~30 min round-trip Adds about half an hour to the Cromwell → Kawarau leg. Worth every minute. Drive carefully on the gravel apron when leaving — there's an honesty mailbox on the post you don't want to clip.
Packing the jars They're glass. Wrap in a tea towel and stow low in a cupboard before Kawarau Gorge's corners — the twists will rattle anything loose.
15:50
30 min · 25 km
DRIVE · GORGE

Into the Kawarau Gorge — Bannockburn → Kawarau Bridge

Bannockburn Rd → SH6 west into the gorge · Route ↗

One of the most dramatic short drives in the country. The road follows the Kawarau River through a narrow schist gorge — turquoise rapids, vine-terraced slopes, decommissioned gold-mining shanties on the cliffs. Take it slow; the corners are tight for a 7 m motorhome and locals overtake aggressively.

16:20
35 min
MUST · SPECTATE

Watch the leap — AJ Hackett Kawarau Bungy Centre

Gibbston Valley, SH6 · jumping until 4 pm, viewing platform free, building open till later · Google Map ↗

This is the world's first commercial bungy jump — AJ Hackett opened it in 1988 off the old Kawarau gold-rush suspension bridge, and you can still watch jumpers go 43 metres into the turquoise river below. Free spectator viewing from a purpose-built platform alongside the bridge; the cafe and shop inside are open later, and there's a small museum about the history of the jump.

The last jump of the day is around 4:00 pm — by arriving 16:20 you'll catch the final 20–30 minutes of jumping (the busiest, since it's "I have to do it today" hour). Wander the suspension bridge itself (free, open 24/7 — it's a heritage crossing); the views down the gorge are spectacular.

Jumping closes at 4 pm sharp Even if you arrive 4:30, you can still cross the bridge, but no more jumpers. Plan for a 16:15–16:20 arrival to actually see people leap. If running late, push hard from the honey shed.
Want to jump yourself? Walk-ups are sometimes possible mid-week (~NZ$305 per person, includes a t-shirt and the video). Book online ahead if anyone is serious — the platform fills fast in summer. Min weight 35 kg, max 235 kg, no upper age limit.

10 min · 8 km
DRIVE

Into vine country — Kawarau → Gibbston Valley

SH6 west, the Gibbston Highway · Route ↗

The road climbs out of the gorge and runs along the south-facing slopes of the Gibbston Valley — New Zealand's highest-altitude wine region, all pinot noir on schist scree. Vines on both sides for the next 8 km.

17:05
40 min
MUST · WINE

Cellar door — Gibbston Valley Winery

1820 SH6, Gibbston Valley · cellar door 10 am–5 pm daily · Google Map ↗

One of the founding wineries of Central Otago, famous for its schist-cave barrel hall — a tunnel dug straight into the mountainside where the pinot ages. The cellar door does flights of 4 wines (~NZ$15–20 per person, redeemable against a bottle purchase). If you can stretch to it, the wine and cheese flight pairs four estate wines with four NZ cheeses — that's your aperitif and first course in one.

The terrace looks out over the home vineyard with the Crown Range behind. They have a large carpark for motorhomes at the far end. The driver can do a non-alcohol flight of grape juice and nibble cheese instead — they'll happily do it.

Closes at 5 pm sharp You're arriving at 5:05 — call ahead from Cromwell (+64 3 442 6910) and ask if they can hold a quick tasting until 5:30. They usually accommodate. If not, drive straight on to Buzzstop and save the wine for tomorrow's Gibbston shuttle.
Plan B — Day 5 wine shuttle If today runs late, skip the cellar door and book a Gibbston wine bus shuttle for tomorrow afternoon — that way the driver can drink too, and you get 3–4 cellar doors instead of one. Around NZ$140 pp from Queenstown.
17:50
25 min · 18 km
DRIVE

To Frankton — Gibbston → Buzzstop

SH6 west, up the Crown Range slope, down into Frankton · Route ↗

One last climb up the Kawarau slope and the whole Wakatipu basin opens up beneath you — first glimpse of Lake Wakatipu in the distance, with the Remarkables running south. Stunning view; don't crash. Drop down into Frankton (Queenstown's airport suburb) and the Buzzstop is on Hansen Rd, ~2 minutes off SH6.

18:15
35 min
MUST · HONEY

The Buzzstop Honey Centre & Café — Frankton

26 Hansen Rd, Frankton · café closes 4 pm Tue, shop and gardens open later · Google Map ↗

An eccentric honey emporium set in a converted 1950s shearing shed. Live observation beehive behind glass, a tasting bar with 20+ varieties of NZ honey (yes, more Mānuka and Rātā, but also weird ones like Pōhutukawa and Tāwari from the far north), an in-house meadery producing honey wine, and a bee-spirit bar with honey-distilled gin and vodka tastings.

Tuesday hours The Buzzstop café closes at 4 pm on Tuesdays (it closes 4:30 every other day). By 18:15 the café is shut, but the shop, honey tasting bar, and bee gardens stay open until around 5 pm. You'll have time to taste and buy, but not to eat. So eat early at Gibbston or save dinner for Queenstown.
What to buy A small Tāwari (gentle butterscotch flavour) and a Pōhutukawa (more robust, slightly salty) round out the Viper's Bugloss from Bannockburn. A flight of honey vodka in a gift box also works as a fun present for the friend back home who waters your plants.
18:50
15 min · 9 km
DRIVE

Last leg into town — Frankton → Queenstown

SH6A along the lakefront → Brecon St → Cemetery Rd · Route ↗

One of the most beautiful 9 km in the country. SH6A traces Lake Wakatipu's edge with the Remarkables to your left, then the road climbs gently into Queenstown's CBD. Right turn onto Brecon, left onto Cemetery — Hampshire Holiday Parks is a few hundred metres up.

19:10
45 min
CHECK IN

Hampshire Holiday Park — Queenstown Lakeview

4 Cemetery Rd, Queenstown · reception 8 am–7 pm · Google Map ↗

The most central holiday park in Queenstown — 10 minutes walk to the lakefront, 5 minutes to the Skyline Gondola. Reception closes at 7 pm, so a 19:10 arrival is just inside the window. Phone ahead from Frankton (+64 3 442 7252) if you're running tight — they'll leave keys/site details in a late-arrival envelope if needed.

Motorhome site tips Some powered sites are tight for a 7 m van — when checking in, ask for a corner pitch or one along the back fence, where the manoeuvring room is best. Levelling blocks may be needed; the ground slopes gently. Dump station is on the way out, do it tomorrow morning.
20:00
90 min
DINNER

Dinner in town — Queenstown CBD

10-min walk down Brecon St into the centre

Two options for tonight:

1. Cook-in feast at the campground (recommended after a long drive) — grill the salmon from Gibbston, real-fruit ice cream from Jones, and a glass of pinot from the winery. Half an hour, then bed. The camp kitchen has a Weber grill outside.

2. Walk into town — try Fergburger (legendary, expect a 30-min queue), or Public Kitchen & Bar on Steamer Wharf for lakefront seafood, or Erik's Fish & Chips for the cheap-and-cheerful option in a converted caravan.

Sunset timing On 10 Nov 2026, Queenstown sunset is ~8:40 pm NZDT with civil twilight to 9:15. If you eat fast, you can catch the alpenglow on the Remarkables from the Queenstown Gardens peninsula at 8:30 — 5 min walk from the centre.

Best station on route: McKeown CardFuel 24/7 — Twizel. Unmanned high-flow truck diesel pump, 24/7, accepts international cards (rare for self-serve in NZ). Pre-paid kiosk system — set $200 max-fill, unused balance refunds within 2–5 days. Roughly 15–25¢/L cheaper than the nearest Cromwell pump.

Backup if Twizel is unreachable: NPD Cromwell (167 McNulty Rd, 6 am–10 pm). Still cheaper than Queenstown; reliable card pumps.

Avoid filling in Queenstown — Frankton stations run 25–40¢/L more than Twizel. Tomorrow's Queenstown driving will burn maybe 5 L; you don't need to fill again until Day 6.

If you fall behind

Skip Clay Cliffs detour — saves 25 min if running late from Omarama.

Eat at Jones Fruit Stall instead of Tarras — they have hot pies and ice cream; saves 30 min if you push past Tarras café's 3 pm closing.

Drop Gibbston cellar door if you'd be arriving after 5 pm. Pick it up tomorrow on a wine shuttle (driver gets to drink too).

Last resort: arrive at Buzzstop before 4 pm (skip Gibbston entirely) — that's the only way to actually eat at the café and do the bee tour.

Most places in the world, a road like this would have one thing on it. In Central Otago, you can crest a tussock pass at lunch, eat real-fruit ice cream from a four-generation orchard at three, watch strangers leap off the world's first bungy bridge at four, sip pinot from a cave carved into schist at five, and taste Viper's Bugloss honey before bed — all in a single afternoon.
