10 · 11 · 2026 — A Tuesday across three regions

Aoraki → Lindis
Gibbston → Queenstown

Roll out of the alpine basin and trade glaciers for golden tussock. Catch your own chinook salmon in the Wairepo canals south of Twizel, climb the otherworldly 971-metre Lindis Pass, graze the Central Otago fruit belt and buy honesty-box honey in Cromwell, watch strangers leap 43 metres off the world's first commercial bungy bridge, and roll into Queenstown by way of a converted shearing-shed honey centre. Sleep two minutes from the lakefront.

★ HEADLINE · FISHING

Catch your own — High Country Salmon

2602 Twizel-Omarama Rd · 3 km south of Twizel on SH8

The main event of the morning. High Country Salmon floats on the glacial waters of Wairepo Arm — a family-owned chinook farm built into the hydro canal system. The Catch-A-Fish Experience runs 9 am – 4 pm, seven days. They supply rod, bait, and a beekeeper-level walk-through; you supply the patience. The salmon are dense, fit fish raised in constant glacial flow — firmer flesh, milder flavour than sea-run.

You pay for what you catch by weight (typical Chinook 2–3 kg, ~NZ$70–110 including processing). They'll bleed, gut, fillet, vacuum-seal and ice-pack your fish in 10 minutes — perfect to slide into the motorhome fridge for tomorrow's dinner. No experience needed. Booking recommended but walk-ins usually accommodated mid-week.

Backup plan if fishing's not for you Skip straight to the Floating Café for sashimi (~NZ$30 for 225 g of belly/loin, made-to-order from a fish caught that morning) or buy fresh fillets from the Fish Shop & Deli. Free fish-feeding off the deck is open all day — donations go to local charity.

DRIVE · VIEWPOINT

Lindis Pass — climb & summit lookout

SH8 · 35 km from Omarama · summit 971 m

The most cinematic stretch of inland New Zealand. SH8 climbs out of the Ahuriri Valley into a saddle of pure golden tussock — no fences, no trees, no houses, just rolling hills that look airbrushed. Pull into the signed Lindis Pass Lookout at the summit (large gravel layby on the left, fits a 7 m motorhome). A 10-minute uphill walk to a higher viewpoint repays the effort with a 360° vista of the Longslip Range and the Otago/Canterbury border.

Mobile signal drops out across the pass — download offline maps before you leave Omarama.

Late lunch — Tarras / in-motorhome

SH8 · Tarras village · 30 km past the pass summit

Drop off the southern flank of the pass into Tarras — a one-shop village with a famously good general store (The Tarras Country Store) selling pies, sandwiches, real-fruit ice creams, and merino-wool souvenirs. MUST · SHOP

Jones Family Fruit Stall — Cromwell

489 SH6, Kawarau Gorge Rd · 3 km east of Cromwell

A Central Otago institution since 1983. Mrs Jones opened it in a Skyline garage by the highway; four decades and three expansions later it pulls travellers off the road for fresh apricots, peaches, nectarines, plums, dried fruit, nuts, honey, and the famous real-fruit ice cream made to order. Open 8 am – 6 pm. Big motorhome-friendly carpark on a gravel apron.

November is the start of the stone-fruit season — cherries are typically late-November/December, so expect early apricots, dried-fruit selection, jams, and excellent ice cream rather than the full December cherry haul. Walk through Mrs Jones' formal English rose garden behind the stall for a quiet 10 minutes while the ice cream is being whirled.

What to take with you A bag of dried Otago apricots, a jar of Pinot fruit port from the Suncrest cellar door inside the stall, and a kiwifruit ice cream for the drive on to the bungy. The dried apricots will travel the rest of the South Island happily.

MUST · HONEY

The Local Honey Shed — Bannockburn, Cromwell

230 Bannockburn Rd, Cromwell · 7 min south of Jones Fruit Stall

A small honesty-box shed set in the Bannockburn vineyard country just south of Cromwell, run by Tim and Zoe — local apiarists harvesting raw Central Otago honey. Four headline varieties on the shelves: Mānuka, Rātā, Wild Thyme (Central Otago's flowering thyme on the dry hillsides) and the prized Viper's Bugloss, a pale blue-flower honey that's nearly impossible to find elsewhere. Open 9 am – 6 pm every day.

No staff on site — it's a self-service shed with tasting spoons, marked prices, and an honesty till. Pay by card (PayWave on the EFTPOS machine), bank transfer, or the phone number on the wall if you need to sort cash/change. Buy a 500 g or 1 kg jar of the Viper's Bugloss for tonight's salmon glaze — it's the one you won't find at home.

Detour cost · 14 minutes round-trip The shed sits 5 km off SH6 on Bannockburn Road. From Jones Fruit Stall: south on SH8B over the dam, right onto Bannockburn Rd, ~5 min in. Easy motorhome access, gravel apron parking. Adds about 14 min vs. driving straight on to Kawarau — worth it for the Viper's Bugloss alone.
Packing tip The jars are glass. Wrap in a tea towel and stow low in a cupboard before the gorge corners — Kawarau's twists will rattle anything loose.

DRIVE

SH6 west — Kawarau Gorge

Cromwell → Gibbston · 27 km of gorge driving

One of the prettiest 25 minutes of highway in New Zealand. SH6 hugs the Kawarau River through a tight schist gorge — vineyards on the south-facing slopes, the river running emerald-green far below. Two-lane, no passing lanes, plenty of slow-moving rental cars; don't push. Watch for the Roaring Meg power station on your right and the suspension footbridge as you approach Gibbston.

★ HEADLINE · SPECTATE

Watch the bungy — AJ Hackett Kawarau Bridge

SH6 · Kawarau Bungy Centre · Gibbston Valley

The world's first commercial bungy site, opened by AJ Hackett and Henry van Asch in November 1988. Today, around 700,000 people have jumped from this 43-metre historic suspension bridge over the Kawarau River. Spectator admission is free, parking is free, and there's a huge cantilevered viewing deck overlooking the bridge — the best free seat in Queenstown's adrenaline circuit.

Jumps run roughly every 90 seconds during peak hours (centre closes at 4 pm, last jump 3:45 pm — arrive by 4:00 latest). Stay 30–45 minutes to see ten or fifteen leaps, including water-touch jumps if the river is at the right level. There's a Bungy Café on site for coffee, and a small retail shop if anyone in the van wants the inevitable T-shirt.

Want to jump? Solo bungy is ~NZ$280 (NZ residents/students) or ~NZ$330 standard; tandem jumps available; minimum age 10. Must book — drop-in slots are rare. Spectating is just as good a story, and quite a bit cheaper.

MUST · HONEY

The Buzzstop Honey Centre & Café

26 Hansen Rd, Frankton · "Country Lane" · 10 min from QT centre

A converted 1950s wool-shearing shed, rescued from 70 years of bird-droppings by third-generation beekeeper Nick Cameron and his team. Now it's a honey centre, café and small artisan hub — bee suits hanging on the wall, frames of beeswax stacked, an indoor bee observatory you can press your nose against. The café closes at 5 pm, so at 17:30 you're aiming for the honey shop and bar — typically open until 7 pm in peak summer (call ahead on the day to confirm).

Pick up a jar of Central Otago thyme honey or wild-clover, taste a flight of mead and honey-liqueur from the bar, and browse the craft shed (honey-turmeric soap, beeswax wraps, soy candles). The Spin Your Own Honey hands-on tour runs at 10 am and 3 pm Mon–Sat through summer — too late for today, but worth knowing for a Day 5 morning if you've got slack in the schedule.

Why this is your last stop before check-in Hansen Rd is in Frankton — 2 min from Queenstown Airport and 10 min from the Hampshire Lakeview check-in. You're already on the way home. Have an early-evening drink and snack here, save dinner appetites for downtown.

§ 02 / THE GIBBSTON CORRIDOR — RANKED

One valley, two flavours

The 30-km stretch between Cromwell and Queenstown packs more sensory variety than any equivalent road in the country: world-first adrenaline at the Kawarau Bridge, and a converted shearing-shed honey centre at the Frankton end. Both are free admission, both pack the kind of story-density that justifies the schedule. The catch is opening hours — the bungy centre closes at 4 pm sharp and the Buzzstop café at 5 pm, with the honey shop running later. The route below is sequenced to make it work.

★ MUST-DO · FREE

Kawarau Bridge Bungy (spectate)

The original. 43 m off the historic 1880 suspension bridge over the Kawarau River. Free admission, free parking, huge cantilevered viewing deck. Watch a dozen jumps in 30 minutes — a story for the rest of the trip.

Arrive 16:00–16:30 to catch the last hour of jumping. The crowd thins after 3 pm, so you'll have space at the rail.

SH6 · Free · 30–45 min · Closes 16:00
★ MUST-DO · TASTY

The Buzzstop Honey Centre

A converted 1950s shearing shed, 2 min from Queenstown airport, 10 min from downtown. Honey tastings, mead flights, an indoor bee observatory, craft-shed make-and-take.

Honey shop and bar typically open until 7 pm in summer — your best bet for an early-evening drink and a jar of Central Otago thyme honey before checking in.

Hansen Rd · Free entry · 45–90 min · Late close

Wine note: Gibbston Valley is one of Central Otago's founding pinot noir regions, but the cellar doors mostly close at 5 pm — leaving the driver to taste alone, and only on the final pour. If you want to do Gibbston wine properly, save it for Day 5 as a half-day shuttle tour from Queenstown — multiple wineries, no driving, full flights for everyone in the van.

The 971-metre Lindis Pass climb is the day's biggest fuel hit — climbing in 4th/5th at 80 km/h burns roughly 15–18 L/100 km until the summit. At Waitomo Twizel's typical Nov 2026 diesel pump (~NZ$2.45–2.55/L), 39 L is roughly NZ$95–100. RUC billed separately by Maui on return (~NZ$8.18/100 km ≈ NZ$21 today).

Waitomo's South Island truckstop network is consistently the cheapest fuel on inland SH8. Unmanned, low-overhead, and built for trucks first — high-flow diesel pumps make a 75-L fill in under three minutes. Fill at Twizel before Omarama — diesel rises 15+ c/L the moment you cross the pass into Otago, with Queenstown the most expensive market in the South Island.

Backup #1 · Twizel

Mobil Twizel

Staffed, central, snacks and toilets. Slightly pricier than Waitomo (5–10 c/L) but useful if Waitomo is busy. 19 Market Place — same complex.

Mid-route · last chance

Caltex Omarama

10 c/L over Twizel but the last station before Cromwell (113 km). Only use if you didn't fuel at Twizel.

Avoid · Tourist pricing

Z / BP Cromwell & Queenstown

Cromwell stations run 20–25 c/L above Twizel; Queenstown is the most expensive market in the South Island. Save the fuel stop for the morning of Day 5 only if you must.
