First and cheapest high-flow stop out of Christchurch. NPD truckstops are tailored to commercial vehicles with high-flow diesel pumps — perfect for the River's 75 L tank. Pay at pump with EFTPOS/credit card. Top up to full here; from here south, pump prices climb 15–25 c/L.

First glimpse — Lake Tekapo Lookout

SH8 descent · just before the village

Pull into the signed lookout on the right as SH8 drops into the basin. The lake's milky-turquoise comes from glacial rock flour suspended in the meltwater — the colour is real, not a filter. Behind the lake: the Two Thumb Range and, far on the horizon, the foothills toward Aoraki/Mount Cook.

OPTIONAL · HIKE

Mt John Summit Walk (recce for tonight)

Trailhead at Tekapo Springs · 5 min walk from Lakes Edge

Climb to the University of Canterbury Observatory at 1,029 m for a 360° look at Tekapo, the basin, and the Southern Alps. Two routes: the lakeside loop (longer, gentler, ~3 hrs return) or the direct climb through pine forest (steeper, ~2 hrs return). The summit café (Astro Café) closes around 5 pm — coffee with the view if you're up by 4:30.

Tonight's preview Walking it in daylight is the safest way to scout the trail. After dark the summit road is closed to private vehicles to protect the observatory — but the walking track stays open and you'll be glad to know where the loose gravel is when you're back here with a head-torch at 9 pm.

OBSERVATION

Sunset over the basin

All directions · west across the lake

Sunset officially 20:35 NZDT. Civil twilight runs to 21:08, nautical to 21:46, and astronomical twilight (true astronomical dark) lands at 22:30. That's your green-light for stargazing — wait for it. Use the hour between dinner and dark to layer up (a fleece + windproof + beanie + gloves; the Mackenzie radiates heat fast at night even in November), prepare a thermos of hot tea, and check torches have red-light mode.

§ 02 / THE HEADLINE EVENT

Stargazing under a 1%-lit waning crescent

November 8, 2026 sits one day before the new moon. The 1%-illuminated waning crescent rises around 04:30 — meaning the entire evening is functionally moonless. The Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve, gazetted in 2012, is the largest dark-sky reserve in the Southern Hemisphere and one of three Gold-tier reserves on Earth. Tekapo measures Bortle Class 2 — among the darkest skies a human can stand under.

With no moon, no light pollution, and (if the weather holds) no cloud, you should be able to see the Milky Way's galactic core arcing south, the Magellanic Clouds, the Southern Cross, and — with averted vision — the faint smudge of the Andromeda Galaxy low on the northern horizon. No telescope required.

★ FIRST CHOICE · SAFEST

Lakes Edge lakefront

Walk 60 seconds from your motorhome to the lakeshore picnic area below the camp. No road to cross, no driving in the dark, no scrambling on tracks. The camp's lighting (in a Dark Sky compliant town) is shielded and minimal.

Bring camping chairs and the spare duvet. You're already home — no shuttle back to a cold van.

0 km · 0 min · Easy · ⚠ Some camp lighting
★★ BETTER · 5-MIN DRIVE

Church of the Good Shepherd

Drive 1.2 km, park in the public lot, walk 100 m to the lakeshore behind the church. The most iconic foreground in New Zealand astrophotography — the stone church silhouetted against the Milky Way.

Expect company (a few photographers and a small guided tour). Keep red-light only and don't use camera-flash — protect everyone's night vision.

1.2 km · 5 min · Easy · Toilets on site
★★★ DARKEST · MOST EFFORT

Cowan's Hill / lakefront pullouts

Drive 5–8 km west along SH8 (Tekapo–Twizel road) and use any of the signed roadside pullouts looking across the lake. Almost zero artificial light; the township vanishes behind a low ridge. This is where serious astrophotographers set up.

Drive there before astronomical dark, park, and stay put. No track scrambling at night.

5–8 km · 10 min · Park & stay · Bring everything

Recommendation: with a 6-berth motorhome and the moonless conditions, the Lakes Edge lakefront wins on the safety-to-sky-quality ratio. You're 60 seconds from a hot drink, the toilet, and bed. The Mackenzie sky is so dark that the gain from driving further is marginal — most of what you can see at Cowan's Hill, you can see from your campsite.

Most areas in the Northern Hemisphere have too much light pollution. The Aoraki Mackenzie Dark Sky Reserve has used special shields and lightbulbs for 30 years to point the light downwards — to preserve the night sky.
