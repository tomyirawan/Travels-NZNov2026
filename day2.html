10:00 AM — 11:15 PM · Hour by Hour

The road south, stop by stop.

258 kilometres along the Inland Scenic Route 72 — through the Rakaia Gorge, past Mount Hutt, into Geraldine and over Burkes Pass into the Mackenzie Basin. The pace builds slowly — gorge, bakery, fuel, lupins, lake — until the day ends under the most protected dark sky on Earth.

10:00DEPART
Departure

Leaving Christchurch behind.

Woolworths CHC Airport → SH73 → SH77 → SH72 (Inland Scenic Route)

Take the inland scenic route — SH73 west out of Christchurch, dropping south onto SH77 then SH72. Slower than SH1 by half an hour, but trades flat coastal plains for the foothills of the Southern Alps and the canyon ahead.

11:2030 min
Must · Lookout

Rakaia Gorge Lookout.

6587 Arundel Rakaia Gorge Road, Mount Hutt · SH72 at the bridge

The first jaw-dropper of the trip. A braided turquoise river cuts through dark rock and wide valleys — the kind of place that makes you pause mid-sentence. 4.7 stars from nearly 300 reviews; locals call it the best stop on the inland route.

What to do (30 min): View from the lookout above the road, then take the short bushwalk down to the riverbed and under the historic Rakaia Gorge Bridge. On clear days the snowy Southern Alps frame the canyon behind. Watch for jet boats and rafters on the river below.

Parking warning: The pull-off is right beside a 70 km/h road with limited space. Indicate well in advance, slow gradually, and back the motorhome in carefully. There's a second pull-off 100 metres closer to the bridge on the opposite side — easier for larger vehicles.

Why detour here Adds 30 min and 30 km to the day, but transforms the morning drive. The braided river is one of Canterbury's signature landscapes — and after Tekapo, you won't pass another one this dramatic.

Widely accepted as the best pie in New Zealand. The salmon-bacon is the cult favourite; the pork-belly with apple sauce is the dark horse. 4.7 stars from 6,500+ reviews — the praise is earned.

A Necessary Reality Check

You're arriving early.

The lupins around Lake Tekapo are one of the most photographed scenes in New Zealand — but they don't run on a tourist schedule. The Russell lupin (Lupinus polyphyllus) flowers from late November to early January, with peak colour around early December.

On 8 November you're at the front edge of the season. Expect early patches starting to colour up — pinks and purples beginning to show against the tussock — rather than the dense purple sea you've seen on Instagram. Patience and a low shooting angle will still get you beautiful frames.

9:45 PM Onwards · The Reason You Came

A sky without limit.

Lake Tekapo sits inside the Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve — one of fewer than two dozen in the world, with strict lighting controls enforced by district bylaw. On a clear November night, the Milky Way is visible to the naked eye in a band so bright it casts a shadow.

Best Free Spot

The lakefront beside the church.

Pioneer Drive · 10-min walk from Lakes Edge

Why here: No fences, no fee, no driving required. You can walk from the holiday park — important after dinner with a drink. The stone church silhouetted against the Milky Way is the iconic shot.

Arrive 9:45 PM for full astronomical darkness. The Milky Way core climbs in the south-east. Give your eyes a full 20 minutes to adapt — no phone screens at full brightness.

Public toilets at the church carpark. Avoid the church carpark lights themselves — walk 30 metres along the foreshore for proper darkness.

✦

Why not Mt John?

The summit road gates close at sunset. You cannot drive the motorhome up after dark. The lakefront delivers 95% of the experience — for free, on foot.

✦

Backup spot

If the church is crowded with photographers, walk 5 min further along Lakeside Drive to the Lake Tekapo Lookout. Wide unobstructed southern sky.

✦

Spending money?

The Dark Sky Project's Mt John summit tour (~NZ$159 pp) includes telescope time. Worth it for serious astronomers — not necessary for great naked-eye and camera viewing.

i.
Red light only

White light destroys night vision and ruins everyone else's photos. Use red cellophane over a phone torch, or set your phone to red-screen mode.

ii.
Dress for 3°C

Even in November, Tekapo nights drop to 3–6°C with windchill on the lakefront. Fleece, beanie, gloves. Worth a trip back to the motorhome to layer up properly.

iii.
Camera settings

ISO 3200–6400, f/2.8 (or your widest), 20–25 sec exposure, manual focus on a bright star, shoot RAW. A tripod is non-negotiable.

iv.
Phone shooters

iPhone 12+ / Pixel 6+ / Galaxy S22+ have astro modes. Brace on a rock or mini-tripod and hold still 30 seconds. The results genuinely surprise.
