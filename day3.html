Day 03 · Monday · 9 November 2026

Pukaki turquoise, alpine king salmon, Aoraki alpenglow.

Roll out of Tekapo and drop south along SH8 — the lake opens turquoise to your right, the Southern Alps rise in the windscreen. Today you'll graze sashimi above Pukaki, then cast a rod and catch your own king salmon in the glacial canals below Twizel for lunch, then turn north onto SH80 toward New Zealand's highest peak. The afternoon climbs three swing bridges to a lake of icebergs at the foot of Aoraki, ends with a moraine view of the Tasman Glacier, and sleeps under the year's darkest sky — exact New Moon, Bortle 2, zero light pollution.

The two salmon stops · why both

Two farms, two experiences

Both farms raise the same NZ Chinook (King) salmon in glacial-fed hydro canals — the cleanest, coldest farming water on earth. They occupy different niches on this route and complement each other beautifully. The Pukaki shop is a 45-min sashimi snack with the lake-and-Aoraki view; High Country is a 90-min sit-down lunch where you catch your own fish.

Same fish, different settings

Both farms grow King Salmon in glacial canal water — firm flesh, clean taste, low oil. Both opened decades ago, both family-owned, both supply the country's top restaurants. The differences are setting, format, and what you do there.

09:4545 minSALMON #1 · MUST

Mt Cook Alpine Salmon Shop — sashimi snack & takeaway

Lake Pukaki Information Centre, SH8/SH80 junction · open 9:30 AM – 4 PM daily

At the southern tip of Lake Pukaki, directly at the SH8/SH80 junction. Their Freshwater King Salmon is raised in canals fed by water flowing straight from the Southern Alps — exceptionally clean, firm, sweet. Most reviewers rate it above top-tier Japanese sushi. Around NZD $100/kg.

What to buy: 250 g sashimi pack (~$25) to share at the outdoor benches with the lake panorama as appetiser, plus a 250 g hot-smoked Pohutukawa pack (~$22) for tonight's BBQ at Glentanner. The Pohutukawa flavour is uniquely NZ — slightly sweet, exclusive to this shop.

The Punatahu Visitor Centre is in the same complex with free maps, toilets ($2 fee — bring coins or card), and a small coffee bar. This is also the official Lake Pukaki Lookout — the wide-angle view stretching north toward Aoraki is one of the most photographed in NZ.

Why now (not later)

The shop closes at 4 PM and you'll be deep in Mt Cook National Park by then. Catch it on the way south at 09:45 — arriving just as they open at 9:30 beats the tour-bus crush around 10:30.

11:001 h 30 minSALMON #2 · LUNCH & FISHING

High Country Salmon — Catch-A-Fish + floating-café lunch

2602 Twizel-Omarama Road · Logans Floating Café 10 AM – 7 PM · Catch-A-Fish 9 AM – 4 PM

The country's only floating café, anchored on the Wairepo Arm of Lake Ruataniwha. Walk along the boardwalk and you're standing directly above the canals where Chinook salmon are raised in constantly flowing glacial water — they grow up fitter, firmer, leaner than sea-run salmon. Same Chinook species as Mt Cook Alpine Salmon, slightly different texture from a different canal system.

The plan for your 90 minutes:

Minutes 0–10 · Park the motorhome (long-vehicle bays available). Confirm your pre-booked Catch-A-Fish slot at the activities area. If you didn't pre-book, walk in and ask — sometimes possible, never guaranteed.

Minutes 10–40 · Catch-A-Fish. One-on-one tuition. No fishing licence needed. Rod, bait and guidance provided. Cast off the platform into the canal pen — fish weigh 3–5 kg, no catch-and-release. Recommend booking two salmon for the group — fishing time scales with fish count (one fish ≈ 30 min, two ≈ 45 min). Kids 12 and under must be with an adult.

Minutes 40–55 · Cleaning & ordering lunch. Staff gill and gut your fish for free; ask for one to be filleted ($12) and packed on ice for dinner, and ask the kitchen to cook the other on-site for the group's lunch (cedar-plank salmon or simple grill — they'll suggest the best prep for a fresh-caught Chinook). Order café food at the same time so everything arrives together. Free fish feeding on the deck if anyone is waiting.

Minutes 55–90 · Lunch on the floating deck. Sashimi platter to share while the planked salmon cooks, then the fresh-caught main course straight to the table. Coffee, chowder, salmon kebabs and salmon pâté available from the cabinet menu for anyone who's already had enough sashimi. Indoor and outdoor seating; outdoor wind can be sharp — pick indoor if it's blowing.

Cost reality-check for a group of 6

Catch-A-Fish: 2 × $30 booking + 2 fish × 4 kg × $35/kg + 1 fillet = ~$340. Café additions (cabinet sushi platter, coffees, sides): ~$15–25/head = $90–150. Total for the stop: ~$430–500 — covers a memorable interactive lunch and stocks tonight's BBQ protein. Less than a Hermitage dinner for 6.

If the kitchen is overwhelmed

Reviewers occasionally note the café can sell out of popular items by noon and may close the hot kitchen early when busy. Backup: have your caught fish filleted and iced instead of cooked on-site, eat sashimi + cabinet food + chowder for lunch, and grill the catch tonight at Glentanner's BBQ pavilion. Still excellent.

SH80, ~13 km from the junction · large bus-friendly lay-by

The single best signposted viewpoint for Lake Pukaki with Aoraki centred in the frame. Upgraded by Boffa Miskell with new viewing decks, paths and signage after the wilding-pine removal — proper big carpark, low fence to the lake, plenty of room for the motorhome plus a couple of buses.

14:403 hrsHIKE · MUST

Hooker Valley Track — three swing bridges to Hooker Lake

White Horse Hill → Hooker Lake · 10.6 km return · 290 m total ascent

The signature walk of the entire South Island. Three swing bridges (including the brand-new 189-metre suspension bridge — at completion, the longest pedestrian bridge in NZ), gravel paths and boardwalks across glacial moraine, milky-blue Mueller Lake, and a final reveal of Hooker Lake with icebergs floating beneath Aoraki/Mt Cook.

Track breakdown

0.0 km · Trailhead — bathrooms, picnic shelter, water tap.
0.4 km · Alpine Memorial — short side trail (5 min) to a moving viewpoint.
1.0 km · First swing bridge — over the Hooker River, Mueller Lake comes into view.
2.2 km · Second (new) swing bridge — the 189-metre flagship.
3.8 km · Third swing bridge & boardwalks — Aoraki dominates the view.
5.3 km · Hooker Lake — turnaround. Icebergs from October–April. Don't wade — water is 3°C.

Gear & pace

Mostly flat (290 m total over 10.6 km), well-formed, easy to moderate. Allow 3 hrs at relaxed group pace with photo and snack stops; fitter walkers do it in 2 hrs 30. Bring layers — alpine weather flips fast. Sunscreen and hat essential. 1 L water per person.

Weather check

If wind exceeds 75 km/h or there's heavy rain, the track gate closes at the first swing bridge. Check forecast at MetService Aoraki/Mt Cook and confirm with Glentanner reception when you check in.

18:1060 minHIKE · MUST

Tasman Glacier Viewpoint & Blue Lakes — alpenglow window

End of Tasman Valley Rd · 1.8 km return, ~85 m ascent, ~350 steps

Short, steep climb up newly rebuilt stone steps to a viewing platform overlooking the milky terminal lake and the retreating tongue of Haupapa/Tasman Glacier — at 22 km, the longest in New Zealand. Floating icebergs visible most days. The glacier recedes 500–800 m every year — this view is genuinely time-limited.

A short 60-metre side trail drops to the Blue Lakes — now actually green (the glacier no longer feeds them; they're rain-fed and have lost the rock-flour blue), but the reflections of the surrounding peaks are still excellent.

Why Tasman after Hooker

Tasman is short but climbs steeply in full sun. Knocking it out at 18:10 catches the warm side-light on the glacier and long shadows in the moraine basin — far better than midday top-light. Legs that have already done Hooker handle 350 steps without complaining.

21:30 – late2–3 hrsSTARGAZING · MUST

Astronomical dark — the year's darkest sky

See the dedicated section below ↓

True astronomical dark starts about 10:30 PM in early November at this latitude. With the New Moon happening at 8:02 PM the same evening, this is literally the darkest natural night sky you will ever see — Bortle 2, UNESCO Dark Sky Reserve, zero moon, zero light pollution, the Milky Way casting visible shadows.

The headline event of the entire trip

Free stargazing — the year's darkest sky

9 November 2026 is not just a "new moon night" — it is the exact moment the moon is in conjunction with the sun, occurring at 08:02 PM NZDT. By the time true astronomical dark arrives (~10:30 PM), the moon is below the horizon and zero percent illuminated. Combined with the Mackenzie Basin's UNESCO Aoraki Dark Sky Reserve status — one of only 22 in the world — you get conditions essentially indistinguishable from a remote desert observatory.

What to look for tonight

Magellanic Clouds (two satellite galaxies of the Milky Way) — only visible from the Southern Hemisphere, easily naked-eye tonight. The Southern Cross high in the south. The Milky Way core sets early but the southern arm is overhead all night. Jupiter and Saturn are bright evening planets in November 2026.

Gear checklist

  • Thermal base layer, fleece, waterproof shell, beanie, gloves — layered, not bulky
  • Camping chair per person + thick wool blanket each
  • Red torch (or phone set to red filter in Accessibility) — preserves night vision
  • Thermos of hot chocolate / tea + small flask of whisky if anyone wants
  • Phone with Stellarium or SkySafari (downloaded offline)
  • For astrophotography: tripod, wide-angle lens (14–24 mm); phone night-mode (3–10 sec) also works
  • Phone airplane mode — saves battery in the cold
The road that defines New Zealand

Photo spots — Aoraki as the backdrop

SH80 from the Pukaki junction to Mt Cook Village is 60 km of straight, near-empty alpine highway with Aoraki growing in the windscreen. Six spots below, ranked from south (start of SH80) to north (Mt Cook Village), with notes on what each one delivers.

Safety first

SH80 is an active highway — most photos involve stopping on the verge, walking to the road shoulder, then darting onto the centre line only when there is no traffic visible in either direction. Two-person spotter rule: one person watches each direction. Never the photographer. Pull the motorhome fully off the seal; never block the lane.

Camera & phone tips

Exposure: Aoraki's snow easily blows out. On a phone, tap-and-hold on Aoraki to lock focus, then drag exposure slider down 1 stop. On a DSLR/mirrorless, expose for the highlights (Aoraki's snow), let the foreground tussock fall darker.

Composition: rule of thirds — peak top on upper-third line, road horizon on lower-third. Foreground interest (a person, the motorhome, a fence post) anchors the eye.

Light: midday (12–3 PM) is the worst — flat, harsh, washed-out blues. 5:30–8:30 PM is gold. Sunrise (6 AM) for cold blue light with Aoraki backlit and silhouetted.

Mt Cook Village pump · Skip

Single self-service pump at the village, car-only access, 30–40 c/L over Twizel. Only worth it if you genuinely run dry. With a Twizel fill you have plenty of range.

Driving tip

The Maui River 6-berth is a Fiat Ducato or Mercedes Sprinter base, ~7 m long, 3 m tall. SH80 has a few 80 km/h zones — don't push 100 in the 80s. Watch for crosswind on the open Pukaki stretch. Use engine braking on short descents to save the discs.

Before you leave Tekapo

Day 3 morning checklist

  • Book Catch-A-Fish online at highcountrysalmon.co.nz — 2 fish for the group, 11:00 AM slot
  • Dump grey/black water at Lakes Edge Holiday Park before departure
  • Refill fresh-water tank (no potable water at Tasman, none on SH80)
  • Plan to refuel at Waitomo Twizel — cheapest on the route
  • Gas isolation valve closed for driving, fridge on 12V mode
  • Cool bag/insulated container ready for ~3–4 kg of fresh salmon coming back from High Country
  • Cash for laundry coins at Glentanner reception (no machines accept notes)
  • Daypacks loaded: 1 L water per person, layers, sunscreen, hat, snacks, charged phone
  • Rain jacket — alpine weather flips fast
  • Check MetService Aoraki/Mt Cook forecast before leaving — wind > 75 km/h closes the Hooker gate
  • Download Stellarium (or SkySafari) offline before you lose signal past Tekapo
  • Confirm Hooker Valley Track full reopening at doc.govt.nz
  • Hot chocolate, blankets, red torch ready for stargazing tonight
