MUST · VIEW

Lake Pukaki Lookout & Visitor Centre

SH8 / SH80 junction · Pukaki Information Kiosk

The reveal. SH8 crests a low ridge and Lake Pukaki — twice the size of Tekapo, an unreal opaque blue from glacial rock flour — opens up with Aoraki/Mount Cook centred on the far horizon, 70 km away across the water. Pull into the large gravel layby on the right where SH80 branches off. Free, big enough for a 7-metre motorhome, and arguably the most photographed view in New Zealand.

The Pukaki Visitor Centre next door has clean toilets, free wifi, a short Ngāi Tahu cultural display, and the famous Mt Cook Alpine Salmon kiosk (try the sashimi cup — NZ$15 for the freshest farmed salmon in the country, raised in the hydro canals you can see across the lake).

Peter's Lookout — the postcard angle

SH80, ~12 km north of SH8 junction

Turn right onto SH80 and run north along Pukaki's western shore. HIKE · MUST

Tasman Glacier Viewpoint & Blue Lakes

Tasman Valley Rd · 8 km gravel from SH80 turnoff

Branch off SH80 onto Tasman Valley Road just before the village (signposted). The first 8 km is sealed; the last stretch is gravel but easy in a motorhome — drive slowly, mind the corrugations. Large unsealed carpark with a shelter and toilets at the trailhead.

The walk: 1.8 km return, ~85 m elevation, ~350 new stone steps to the top. Allow 40–60 min including time at the viewpoint. From the lookout you see the milky terminal lake, floating icebergs, the moraine wall, and the retreating tongue of Haupapa/Tasman Glacier — at 22 km, the longest in New Zealand. The glacier recedes 500–800 metres a year, so this view is genuinely time-limited.

A 60-metre side trip drops to the Blue Lakes (now actually green — the glacier no longer feeds them, so they're rain-fed and have lost the rock-flour blue). The reflections of the surrounding peaks are still excellent.

Why before Hooker, not after The Tasman track climbs steps in full sun and there's no shade. Knock it out at midday when it's already exposed anyway, and save the longer, more contemplative Hooker walk for late afternoon when the light is golden and the crowds thin.

★ HEADLINE HIKE

Hooker Valley Track — to Hooker Lake

White Horse Hill → Hooker Lake · 10.6 km return

The walk: 10.6 km return, ~200 m total elevation, three suspension bridges, an alpine memorial, two glacial lakes (Mueller and Hooker), and the closest unguided view of Aoraki/Mount Cook from any walking track in New Zealand. Mostly flat boardwalk and gravel, the few climbs are gentle.

Reopening status: The new 189-metre suspension bridge over the Hooker River opened in late July / early August 2026, after a year of partial closure. By 9 November, the full track to Hooker Lake should be open again. Confirm at the DOC trailhead board before setting off — if the bridge is closed for any reason, turn around at Mueller Lake Lookout (~1 hr return) and the day still works.

Pace: Allow 3.5 hours. Out in 1h45 at a relaxed photo-stopping pace, 30 min at the lake (icebergs floating, picnic tables, Aoraki framed directly behind), back in 1h15. Doable for almost any fitness level. The end of the track is the closest a walking trail comes to NZ's highest peak.

Weather brief Aoraki/Mount Cook makes its own weather. Strong wind, snow, hail — all possible in a single November afternoon. Pack a windproof, fleece, hat, gloves, 1.5 L water per person, snacks, sunscreen (alpine UV is brutal even when cloudy). Boots over sneakers if you've got them; the new bridge and boardwalks are slip-resistant but the gravel sections after rain are not.

★ HEADLINE

Stargazing — Lake Pukaki under a New Moon

Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve

Tonight is the calendar's gift: 9 November is the exact New Moon, illumination 0%. With no moon in the sky at all and Pukaki measuring Bortle 2, this is statistically one of the two or three darkest, best-stargazing nights of the entire year in one of the darkest places on the planet. Three free, safe spots are detailed below.

§ 02 / The headline event

The exact New Moon, the darkest reserve, the bluest lake

Last night you had a 1%-lit crescent. Tonight you have the New Moon itself. The Moon reaches new at 20:02 NZDT on 9 November 2026, which means from astronomical dark (22:32) right through to sunrise, the sky carries zero lunar light. Combined with Lake Pukaki's location at the absolute heart of the 4,300 km² Aoraki Mackenzie International Dark Sky Reserve — a Gold-tier reserve, one of three on Earth, Bortle Class 2 — tonight is functionally the darkest sky you can stand under in the Southern Hemisphere.

What you should see, no telescope required: the Milky Way's galactic core stretched across the sky from southwest to northeast, the Large and Small Magellanic Clouds high overhead (nearby galaxies invisible from the Northern Hemisphere), the Southern Cross and its pointers, the Coalsack dark nebula as a black hole within the Milky Way, Jupiter and Saturn if they're up. With averted vision and patience, the faint band of zodiacal light may show along the ecliptic after astronomical dark.

★ FIRST CHOICE · SAFEST

Glentanner lakefront

Walk 60 seconds from your motorhome to Lake Pukaki's edge below the camp. No driving in the dark, no track scrambling, no road to cross. You face south and west across the lake — directly into the Milky Way arc and the southern celestial pole. Bring camping chairs and the spare duvet.

Glentanner is genuinely dark and shielded; the few security lights are downward-facing per Dark Sky compliance.

★★ BETTER · 5-MIN DRIVE

Peter's Lookout (SH80)

Drive 10 km south on SH80 back to the same gravel pullout you stopped at this morning. Higher elevation than the lakefront, wider sky to the south, and a foreground of Aoraki silhouetted against the southern Milky Way. Most other tourists here will have headed back to Tekapo or Twizel — you'll often have it to yourself.

Park well clear of the road. Use hazards briefly when arriving, red-light only once you're stationary.

★★★ DARKEST · MOST EFFORT

Lake Pukaki Reserve / The Pines

DOC freedom-camping reserve on the south shore of Lake Pukaki, ~25 min south on SH8. Two long-drop toilets, zero artificial light, lake views in every direction. Photographers' favourite. You can shoot from the gravel beach with the lake as a mirror foreground.

Drive there before astronomical dark, park, and stay put. Don't walk along the shoreline at night — loose rocks at the waterline.

25 km · 25 min · Park & stay · Bring everything

Recommendation: with Day 2 already invested in Tekapo stargazing, tonight is the night to go a little further. Peter's Lookout is the sweet spot — 10 minutes from Glentanner, no track scrambling, elevated horizon, Aoraki as silhouette, and you're back in the motorhome by midnight. If you're tired or the weather isn't perfect, the Glentanner lakefront is still Bortle 2 and 99% as good.
