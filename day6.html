⟡ The Headline Decision ⟡

When to enter the cave of rushing water

The glowworms glow all the time — the caves are pitch-black year-round, so the worms themselves don't care what your watch says. But three other factors shift dramatically with time of day, and on 12 November the sun sets at 8:38 PM with twilight lasting until ~9:30 PM — so an early-evening tour still gets daylight on the lake outbound and a magical dusk return.

Eye-adaptation: RealNZ guides and Maui's own travel guide both recommend late-afternoon or evening tours — your eyes have already begun dark-adapting, so the grotto's glow appears denser and brighter when you arrive.

Lake scenery: the 45-minute catamaran crossing detours past landscape features the captain points out — you want this in daylight or golden light, not full dark.

Crowds: the noon and early-afternoon slots are clogged with Milford Sound day-tripper coaches arriving back into Te Anau. Late afternoon is calm.

"Te Ana-au" — cave of rushing water. The cave was named first; the lake (and then the town) took the name from it. No European recorded entering it until 1948.

Main Objectives

i

Cruise the Wakatipu shore

Take the spectacular SH6 cliff-road south through Devil's Staircase, with multiple lake-and-Remarkables panoramas — this is one of the South Island's quietly great drives, and the morning light works in your favour heading south.

ii

Slow stops, no detours

Stay on the SH6 → SH97 → SH94 corridor with a single linear flow — Kingston, Garston, Five Rivers, Mossburn — no back-tracking. Each pause earns its keep: coffee, honey, farm animals, or a viewpoint.

iii

End under glowing stars

Arrive Te Anau by mid-afternoon — settle the motorhome, refuel diesel, light snack, then the evening's headline: the 5:45 PM RealNZ Glowworm Caves tour by boat across Lake Te Anau, with daylight out and golden-hour return.

Must Stop Lookout

Devil's Staircase Lookout Point

SH6 · 35 km south of Queenstown · roadside pull-off

The road climbs and twists above Lake Wakatipu's eastern cliff in a series of switchbacks the early miners named the Devil's Staircase. The pull-off opens onto a sweeping view down the lake toward the Remarkables — emerald water, dry-tussock hills, snow-streaked peaks. Two lay-bys here: if the first is full, the second is moments later.

Heads-up: the road narrows as you climb. In the motorhome, take the corners gently and don't be afraid to pull over and let faster traffic past.

Kingston & the Flyer Cafe

SH6 · southern tip of Lake Wakatipu · 50 km from Q'town

Kingston sits at Wakatipu's southern toe, where the lake ends and the road bends inland. The 1927-built Kingston Flyer steam train is parked beside the heritage station — old carriages and the original platform still stand, good for photos even when not running. Grab a coffee or hot chocolate at the Flyer Cafe & Bar a short walk away (note: cafe's main hours are afternoon onwards, but morning hot-drinks are usually available; if closed, the Kingston Store on SH6 is reliable).

Optional Shop

Garston Hunny Shop

SH6 · Garston · NZ's most-inland village

A tiny family operation just off the highway — five or more varieties of New Zealand honey on free tasting: clover, manuka, wild thyme (a local favourite), honeydew, plus lozenges and honey-based skincare. Friendly, unrushed, and the staff will tell you straight which honeys travel well in luggage. Worth 15–20 minutes and a jar or two for the road ahead.

Must Stop Lunch

Five Rivers Cafe — lunch

SH6/SH97 junction · the natural turnoff to Te Anau

Five Rivers Cafe sits exactly where you turn right off SH6 onto SH97 toward Mossburn — making it both a flavour-stop and a navigational marker. Indoor & outdoor seating with mountain views, a small local art gallery inside, and a paddock of friendly farm animals (alpaca, goats, sheep, mini-horse, chickens) right behind the cafe — kids and adults alike enjoy feeding them. Eggs Benedict and the pancakes get consistent praise; coffee is good. Open 9 AM–3 PM, so you'll arrive within window.

Why here, not earlier: you've driven 90 km from Queenstown, the legs need a stretch, and this is your last sit-down option before Te Anau — Mossburn (next town) has fuel and a small shop but limited food.

Must Stop 360° Lookout

Te Anau Lions Lookout

3 km off SH94 · Ramparts Road · first view of Fiordland

Just before town, a signposted side-road climbs to a small lookout giving a 360° panorama: Lake Te Anau spread out below, the Kepler and Murchison ranges rising behind, and the farmland of central Southland rolling away east. Almost nobody stops here — you may have it to yourself. The side-road is narrow and unsealed near the top; take it slow in the motorhome and be ready to give way.

The Headline Pre-book online

Te Anau Glowworm Caves

RealNZ · catamaran across the lake → guided cave + grotto punt → return by 8 PM

The headline of the day. A 45-minute catamaran cruise crosses Lake Te Anau to the western shore — a coastline accessible only by boat. At Cavern House, your guide leads a small group (12 max per guide) into the limestone cave system; rushing underground water has carved the passages, which are geologically young — only ~12,000 years old, and still being shaped right now. The walk ends at a hidden grotto where you board a small punt in pitch silence — and overhead, thousands of bioluminescent Arachnocampa luminosa larvae light the ceiling like a private star field.

The 5:45 PM tour is the sweet spot: outbound across the lake in clear daylight, then the cave for 30 minutes in total darkness while your eyes are already adjusting to evening light, then the return crossing in golden hour — sun setting behind the Murchison Mountains, lake water gone metallic.

Bring: warm fleece or sweater, non-slip walking shoes, a waterproof if rain is forecast. Leave behind: all cameras and phones — no photography permitted in the glowworm grotto, even silent mode (flash and screen-light disturb the worms and ruin everyone else's dark-adaptation). The tour returns to the wharf around 8:00 PM.

Must Book Dinner

Redcliff Restaurant & Bar

12 Mokonui Street · open until 11:30 PM · book ahead

The standout restaurant in Te Anau, occupying a vintage settler's cottage and serving classic South Island plates — venison, hare croquettes, blue cod, lamb. They fill up every night — book a few days ahead and ask for an 8:30 PM table so you're not rushing the post-tour walk back. A warm, characterful room, with original LOTR cast signatures on the wall from the filming days. Wine list is solid; the Central Otago pinots are local.

Backup options if Redcliff is full or too late: Sandfly Café (closes earlier, casual), or the Fat Duck Bar & Eatery on the lakefront for relaxed gastropub fare with later hours.

Fuel plan

Recommended refuel

Caltex Te Anau · Luxmore Drive

The best option for the Maui River motorhome: manned, pay-after-fuelling, open 6 AM–9 PM daily, with a generous forecourt that easily handles a 7.25 m vehicle. High-flow diesel pump suitable for trucks and large RVs. Sits right at the SH94 entry to town, so it's a no-detour stop on arrival.

From here you'll have a full tank for tomorrow's Milford return — critical, as there is no fuel between Te Anau and Milford Sound (~240 km round-trip on SH94).
